# What is information security?

Information security is about protecting the information your business uses every day – and the systems that store and process it – so that your organization can operate safely and reliably.

This document explains information security in business terms, not technical jargon, so non‑security staff can understand what it is, why it matters, and how it connects to their daily work.

---

## 1. What we mean by “information”

In this guide, “information” includes any data that has value to your business, in any format:

- **Digital data**
  - Files, emails, databases, applications, logs.  
  - Data stored on laptops, servers, phones, cloud services and backups.

- **Paper records**
  - Contracts, invoices, HR files, printed reports and notes.

- **Knowledge and process**
  - Procedures, know‑how, internal documentation, designs and intellectual property.

Protecting information is not only about technology. It also involves people and processes.

---

## 2. Short definition (business‑friendly)

A practical way to define information security for small and medium businesses is:

> Information security is the practice of protecting the **confidentiality**, **integrity** and **availability** of the information and systems your business relies on.

In plain language:

- Keeping sensitive information private and controlled.  
- Keeping information accurate and trustworthy.  
- Keeping systems and data usable when needed.

This is the same CIA triad explained in `basic-principles.md`, applied to your business context.

---

## 3. Common threats to small and medium businesses

Typical threats include:

- **Phishing and social engineering**
  - Fake emails or messages tricking staff into revealing passwords, sending money or opening malicious attachments.

- **Malware and ransomware**
  - Malicious software that steals information, encrypts files for ransom or uses your systems to attack others.

- **Account compromise**
  - Attackers using stolen or guessed passwords to log into email, cloud services or remote access systems.

- **Insider mistakes**
  - Accidental data exposure (for example, emailing a file to the wrong recipient, misconfiguring access to a shared folder).

- **Lost or stolen devices**
  - Laptops, phones or USB drives with sensitive data left in public places or stolen.

- **Third‑party incidents**
  - Breaches or outages at vendors, cloud services or service providers that hold your data or have access to your systems.

These threats can impact businesses of any size, and attackers increasingly target smaller organizations because they are often less protected.

---

## 4. Why information security matters for your business

Information security is not just an IT problem. It is a **business risk** issue.

Poor security can lead to:

- **Financial loss**
  - Direct losses (fraud, ransomware payments, incident response costs).  
  - Indirect losses (downtime, lost sales, penalties).

- **Reputational damage**
  - Loss of customer trust if you mishandle data or suffer a public incident.

- **Legal and regulatory issues**
  - Non‑compliance with privacy laws, contractual obligations or sector requirements.

- **Operational disruption**
  - Inability to access systems and data needed to run everyday operations.

Good information security, on the other hand:

- Reduces the likelihood and impact of incidents.  
- Protects customer and employee information.  
- Supports contracts and partnerships where security is a requirement.  
- Builds trust with customers, partners and regulators.

---

## 5. Information security vs. “cybersecurity”

The terms are often used interchangeably, but it is useful to distinguish them:

- **Information security**
  - Focuses on protecting information (digital and physical), regardless of the medium.  
  - Covers people, processes and technology, including paper, physical access and organizational policies.

- **Cybersecurity**
  - Focuses more on protecting digital systems and networks connected to the internet or other networks.

In practice, for small and medium businesses:

- Most of your efforts will be “cybersecurity” activities (for example, protecting networks, devices and cloud services).  
- But you should also consider physical security, paper documents and vendor risk as part of a **broader information security** program.

This guide uses “information security” in the broader sense, but many controls also fall under “cybersecurity”.

---

## 6. People, process and technology

Effective information security is built on three pillars:

- **People**
  - Staff, contractors and management.  
  - Training, awareness and clear responsibilities.  
  - Culture that encourages reporting issues instead of hiding them.

- **Process**
  - Policies, procedures and ways of working (for example, how to onboard staff, approve access, handle incidents).  
  - Governance and decision‑making about risk.

- **Technology**
  - Tools and configurations (for example, firewalls, antivirus, patching, backups, logging, access control).

All three are necessary. Technology can support good processes and help people make safer choices, but cannot replace them.

## 7. Risk‑based approach (keep it realistic)

Information security is not about eliminating all risk; that is impossible. Instead, it is about **managing risk** to a level your business can accept.

For small and medium businesses, a risk‑based approach means:

- Identifying your most important assets and processes (what would hurt the most if lost, stolen or unavailable).  
- Understanding the most likely and most damaging threats to those assets.  
- Choosing a **small number of high‑impact controls** that give good protection for reasonable cost and effort.  
- Improving gradually over time, not trying to do everything at once.

The rest of this repository is organized to support this approach:

- **Foundations** – concepts and frameworks (NIST CSF, CIS Controls) to structure your thinking.  
- **Where to start** – a 30‑day action plan and other early steps.  
- **Practical controls** – concrete actions non‑specialists can take.  
- **Frameworks explained** – how your actions align with recognized standards.

## 8. How non‑security staff should use this

If you are not a security specialist but have responsibilities that touch information or systems, you can use this guide to:

- Understand **why** certain rules and controls exist (for example, MFA, backups, Wi‑Fi settings).  
- Find **step‑by‑step instructions** in the “practical controls” sections.  
- Explain to colleagues and management, in simple terms, what your organization is doing to protect its information.

You do not need to memorize every concept. It is enough to:

1. Keep the CIA triad (confidentiality, integrity, availability) in mind.  
2. Know where to find the relevant sections in this repository when you face a specific question or task.  
3. Ask for help from IT or security when something is unclear or looks suspicious.

Information security is a shared responsibility. This guide is designed to make that responsibility **understandable and actionable** for everyone in the organization, not just technical staff.
