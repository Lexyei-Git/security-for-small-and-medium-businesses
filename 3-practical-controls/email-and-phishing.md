# Email and phishing

Email is one of the main ways attackers target small and medium businesses.  
A single malicious message can lead to stolen credentials, fraudulent payments or malware infections.

This document explains, in practical terms, how to reduce email risk and help staff recognize and handle phishing attempts.

---

## 1. What phishing is (in business terms)

Phishing is when an attacker sends a message (usually email, but also SMS or chat) pretending to be someone you trust in order to make you:

- Reveal sensitive information (passwords, banking details, customer data).  
- Click on a malicious link that steals credentials or installs malware.  
- Open a malicious attachment.  
- Approve a fraudulent payment or change in banking details.

Common examples in small and medium businesses:

- “Your account will be closed, click here to verify your details.”  
- “Invoice attached, payment overdue” from an unknown or spoofed supplier.  
- “Urgent: CEO request – buy gift cards / send payment now” (business email compromise).

---

## 2. Why email and phishing matter for SMBs

For many small and medium businesses:

- Email is the main communication channel with customers, suppliers and banks.  
- A successful phishing attack can lead to:
  - Theft of money via fraudulent transfers or fake invoices.  
  - Compromise of email accounts and other systems.  
  - Data breaches involving customer or employee information.  
  - Ransomware incidents that stop operations.

Attackers often see small businesses as easier targets because they have fewer dedicated security resources, but still hold valuable data and money.

---

## 3. Typical signs of a phishing email

You can train staff to look for common red flags. One suspicious sign does not always mean phishing, but several together should trigger caution.

Key red flags:

- **Urgent or threatening language**
  - “Act now or your account will be closed”, “Immediate action required”, “Final notice”.  

- **Unexpected attachments or links**
  - Attachments you were not expecting, especially with unusual file types (for example, `.exe`, `.js`, `.scr`, or password‑protected archives).  
  - Links that do not match the displayed text, or point to unfamiliar or misspelled domains.

- **Requests for sensitive information**
  - Asking you to provide passwords, MFA codes, full credit card numbers or confidential data by email or via a link.  
  - Login pages that appear after clicking a link from an email that was not expected.

- **Suspicious sender details**
  - Email addresses that are close to, but not exactly, your real suppliers or colleagues (for example, `@examp1e.com` instead of `@example.com`).  
  - Messages that pretend to be from inside the company but come from an external address.

- **Poor quality or inconsistent details**
  - Spelling and grammar errors, low‑quality or distorted logos, formatting that does not look like legitimate corporate communication.  

- **Out‑of‑context messages**
  - Invoices for products you never ordered, receipts for things you did not buy, or instructions from a “manager” that were never discussed before.

---

## 4. Simple rules for staff: what to do and not do

Most staff are not security experts. Give them simple rules they can remember.

Suggested “do” rules:

- **Do stop and think** if an email feels unusual, urgent or out of context.  
- **Do check the sender** by looking at the full email address, not just the display name.  
- **Do verify requests** for payments, changes in banking details or sensitive data using a trusted channel (for example, phone number you already know, internal chat).  
- **Do report suspicious emails** to your IT contact or manager, using a simple method (for example, forward to a dedicated address or click a “Report phishing” button if available).

Suggested “do not” rules:

- **Do not click** on links or open attachments in suspicious or unexpected messages.  
- **Do not enter your password** or MFA code on websites reached from suspicious emails.  
- **Do not reply** to messages asking for passwords or financial details. Legitimate organizations should not ask for these by email.  
- **Do not ignore** suspected phishing; always report it so the organization can respond.

---

## 5. Technical measures to support users

Technology alone cannot stop all phishing, but it can block many malicious messages and make red flags more visible.

Practical controls for small and medium businesses:

- **Email filtering and spam protection**
  - Use reputable email filtering to block known malicious senders, attachments and links.  
  - Turn on built‑in phishing and malware protection offered by your email provider.

- **External sender tagging**
  - Configure email to mark messages from outside the organization (for example, with a banner such as “External sender – use caution”).  
  - This helps staff recognize messages that **pretend** to be internal.

- **Attachment and link scanning**
  - Use features that scan or sandbox attachments and links before they reach users, when available in your email platform or security tools.

- **Domain protection**
  - Implement SPF, DKIM and DMARC for your email domain to make it harder for attackers to spoof your domain in phishing emails.  

- **Endpoint protection**
  - Ensure up‑to‑date antivirus/anti‑malware is installed on all business devices to help block malicious downloads.

- **MFA and strong authentication**
  - Enforce MFA on email and key systems so that even if a password is stolen via phishing, the attacker has a harder time logging in.

---

## 6. Awareness and training (keep it practical)

Awareness is not a one‑time presentation. Short, regular reminders are more effective.

Suggestions for small and medium businesses:

- **Short, focused messages**
  - Periodically send short tips (for example, monthly) about how to spot phishing signs, with real‑world examples.  

- **Use real examples (with redacted data)**
  - Show anonymized examples of phishing emails received by your organization and highlight the red flags.

- **Simulation, if feasible**
  - If you have access to phishing simulation tools (some email platforms provide them), use occasional, simple tests to reinforce awareness and measure improvement.

- **Onboarding**
  - Include basic phishing and email security guidance in onboarding for all new staff, not only IT roles.
    
Make it clear that **reporting** suspicious emails is a positive behavior, even if they turn out to be legitimate.

---

## 7. What to do if someone clicks

Even with good controls and training, someone will eventually click or respond to a phishing email. The goal is to **limit the damage** and learn from the incident.

High‑level steps:

1. **Stay calm and report immediately**
   - The person should notify the designated contact (IT, manager, or service provider) as soon as possible and explain what happened.

2. **Contain the incident**
   - If malware is suspected, disconnect the affected device from the network and Wi‑Fi.  
   - Change passwords for any accounts that may have been exposed and enforce MFA if not already in place.

3. **Check email rules and forwarding**
   - For compromised email accounts, check for malicious forwarding rules or auto‑reply changes set up by the attacker.

4. **Contact relevant parties**
   - For fraudulent payments or banking details changes, contact your financial institution immediately.  
   - If personal data may have been exposed, consider legal and regulatory obligations to notify affected individuals or authorities.

5. **Review and improve**
   - After the incident, review what happened, adjust training and technical controls, and update procedures to reduce the chance of similar events.

---

## 8. Minimal checklist for email and phishing

Use this checklist to quickly assess your current state:

- [ ] We use spam and phishing filters for our business email.  
- [ ] External senders are clearly labelled in incoming emails.  
- [ ] Staff have received simple, written guidance on how to spot phishing and what to do.  
- [ ] There is a clear way for staff to report suspicious emails.  
- [ ] MFA is enabled on email accounts and other critical systems.  
- [ ] We have basic procedures for responding if someone clicks on a phishing link or opens a malicious attachment.  
- [ ] We periodically remind staff about phishing risks and red flags.  
- [ ] We understand which roles are most at risk (for example, finance, executives, HR) and pay extra attention to them.

If several boxes are unchecked, treat them as priorities for your next 30–90 days of security work.
