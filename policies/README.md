# Security policies for small and medium businesses

This folder contains **starter security policies** written in clear, plain language for small and medium businesses.

They are designed to be:

- Understandable by managers and non‑technical staff.
- Aligned with common frameworks (NIST CSF, CIS Controls) and Canadian small‑business guidance.
- A practical baseline that you can adapt, not final legal documents.

---

## 1. What these policies are (and are not)

These policies are:

- A **minimum set of rules and responsibilities** that support the practical controls described in this repository.
- Written to be short, focused and easy to explain to employees.
- A starting point for a more formal security program as the organization grows.

They are not:

- A replacement for legal advice or regulatory guidance.
- A guarantee of compliance with any specific standard or law.
- A static set of documents that never change.

Managers should treat them as **living documents**, updated as risks, systems and regulations evolve.

---

## 2. Policies included in this folder

Recommended starter policies:

- `acceptable-use-policy.md`  
  Rules for how users can and cannot use company devices, systems, networks and data.

- `passwords-and-mfa-policy.md`  
  Requirements for passwords, passphrases and multi‑factor authentication (MFA) on company accounts.

- `backup-and-data-protection-policy.md`  
  Expectations and responsibilities for backing up and protecting critical business data.

- `email-internet-and-phishing-policy.md`  
  Rules for using email and the internet safely, and how to handle phishing attempts.

- `incident-reporting-policy.md`  
  When and how users must report suspected security incidents.

- `vendor-and-third-party-security-policy.md`  
  Basic security expectations for vendors and third parties that access company systems or data.

These cover the **5–7 core policy areas** that many small‑business guides recommend as a starting point.

## 3. How managers should choose and adapt policies

A simple approach for managers:

1. **Start from your risks and priorities**  
   - Use `2-where-to-start/basic-risk-assessment.md` and `simple-asset-inventory.md` to understand what matters most.  
   - Focus first on policies that address your highest‑priority risks (for example, email/phishing, passwords and MFA, backups).

2. **Select the policies you need now**  
   - For most small and medium businesses, all six starter policies are relevant.  
   - If your environment is very simple, you can start with:
     - Acceptable use  
     - Passwords and MFA  
     - Email/Internet/Phishing  
     - Backup and data protection  
     - Incident reporting

3. **Adapt wording to your organization**  
   - Replace `[Company Name]`, `[Owner/Role]` and `[Contact/Team]` with real names or roles.  
   - Adjust examples and technologies to match what you actually use.  
   - Keep language plain and avoid unnecessary legal jargon.

4. **Check alignment with laws and contracts**  
   - For sectors with specific requirements (for example, financial, health, public), involve legal or compliance advisors.  
   - Make sure policies do not conflict with employment contracts, collective agreements or supplier contracts.

## 4. How to approve policies inside the company

For a small or medium business, a **simple governance process** is enough:

1. **Draft**  
   - IT, security or an internal champion prepares drafts using these templates and the organization’s risk assessment.

2. **Review**  
   - Share drafts with:
     - Management / leadership.  
     - HR (for employee‑related aspects).  
     - Legal or external advisors if needed.  
     - Any department that will be heavily affected (for example, Finance for vendor policy).

3. **Approve**  
   - Leadership formally approves the policies (for example, in a management meeting or via written sign‑off).  
   - Record:
     - Policy name and version.  
     - Approval date.  
     - Approving role(s) or body.

4. **Publish**  
   - Store policies in an accessible location:
     - Intranet or internal wiki.  
     - Shared drive or document management system.  
     - Employee handbook or HR portal.

This process ensures policies have **visible leadership support**, which is critical for adoption.

## 5. How to roll out policies to staff

Policies are only useful if people know and follow them. A practical rollout process:

1. **Communicate the “why”**  
   - Explain that the goal is to protect the business, jobs and customers, not to make life harder.  
   - Link policies to real risks (for example, phishing, ransomware, fraud).

2. **Training / briefing**
   - Hold a short session (virtual or in person) to walk through the main points:
     - What is new.  
     - What is expected of staff.  
     - How to get help or ask questions.

3. **Acknowledgment**
   - Ask staff to confirm that they have read and understood the policies:
     - Via an HR system or e‑signature tool, or  
     - By signing a simple acknowledgment form.

4. **Make policies easy to find**
   - Keep all policies in one obvious place, with clear names.  
   - Link to them from onboarding materials and internal communications.

5. **Reinforce over time**
   - Include policy reminders in periodic training and awareness campaigns (for example, annual refreshers, phishing awareness month).

## 6. Keeping policies current

Policies should be reviewed and updated regularly:

- **Review frequency**
  - At least **once per year**, and  
  - After major changes (for example, new systems, major incidents, regulatory changes).

- **Review triggers**
  - Significant incident or near‑miss.  
  - New cloud services or major outsourcing.  
  - Mergers, acquisitions or major organizational changes.

- **What to check**
  - Do the policies still match how the organization actually works?  
  - Are there gaps revealed by recent incidents or audits?  
  - Do any requirements no longer make sense or need simplification?

Track versions and changes so you know:

- Which version is current.  
- When each version was approved.  
- What changed between versions.

## 7. Quick checklist for managers

Use this as a quick governance checklist:

- [ ] We have identified which of the starter policies are needed now.  
- [ ] Drafts have been adapted with our company name, roles and technologies.  
- [ ] Leadership (and, where needed, legal/HR) has reviewed and approved the policies.  
- [ ] Policies are published in a place that staff can easily access.  
- [ ] Staff have been informed and have acknowledged the key policies.  
- [ ] We have decided how often we will review and update policies.  
- [ ] Policy content is consistent with our practical controls and risk assessment.

If several boxes are unchecked, use this README as a step‑by‑step guide to close the gaps and turn these templates into a functioning policy framework for your organization.
